Camp Ketosis Devlog: Making a Ludum Dare Game in 72 Hours

How my teammate and I went from Friday-night theme reveal to Sunday submission — what broke, what clicked, and what I'd do differently next jam.

Ludum Dare 57 dropped its theme on Friday night: Depths. My teammate and I had about 30 seconds of silence on Discord before the brainstorm kicked off. This is the part I love about game jams — the constraints are brutal and the deadline is real, so every decision has actual weight.

We'd jammed together before, so we had a rhythm: I own all the engineering, they handle art and audio. That split works for us because it means neither person is blocked waiting on the other. By the time I have something running, there's already concept art to drop in.

Finding the Concept

"Depths" is one of those themes that sounds specific but opens up wide fast. You can go underwater, underground, psychological, metaphorical. We burned through about a dozen ideas before landing on something that felt like a game rather than a tech demo: a premise that let us build a tight core loop without requiring systems we couldn't finish in 72 hours.

The rule I try to follow in jams: the fun has to be achievable by Saturday night. If the core mechanic isn't working and enjoyable by then, you're not polishing — you're still designing. That's the fastest way to submit something that doesn't feel finished.

Day 1 — Core Mechanic First

I opened Unity, set up version control, and pushed a skeleton project before touching any actual gameplay code. Boring but essential — nothing kills a jam faster than a corrupted project at hour 60.

The first few hours went into the player controller and whatever the core interaction was. I'm a big believer in getting the feel right early. If movement is sluggish or inputs feel off, it colors everything else. Players will forgive a lot of rough edges if the thing they're doing feels good.

By the time I went to sleep Saturday morning, the core loop was running. Nothing polished, placeholder art everywhere, but the idea was provable. That's the target for end of day 1.

Day 2 — Content and Integration

My teammate dropped the first batch of sprites and audio in the morning. This is always a huge morale moment — suddenly the game looks like a game instead of a programmer-art proof of concept. It also usually triggers a round of scope cuts, because you realize how long proper integration takes.

We cut two planned features on day 2. I've learned to be ruthless about this. A half-implemented feature almost always hurts the game more than it helps — it draws attention to what's missing instead of what's there. Better to have five things that work than eight things that kind of work.

Audio went in late Saturday afternoon. Audio is one of those things that game devs chronically underestimate. A sound effect and a music track made the game feel 40% more complete without a single line of code changing.

Day 3 — The Crunch Hours

Sunday is different from the other days. Everything that was going to go wrong has already gone wrong once. You're not writing new features, you're fixing the breaks from the sprint.

The bugs that show up on submission day are always the ones you introduced in the last hour you should have been testing. I've accepted this as a constant of game jams. The discipline is knowing when to stop adding things and start playing through the game as a stranger would.

We submitted about an hour before the deadline. The build worked. The game had a beginning, middle, and end. That's a win.

What I'd Do Differently

  • Scope the tutorial earlier. We added the onboarding flow in the last four hours, which meant it got the least design attention of anything in the game.
  • More playtesting checkpoints. We were so heads-down building that we didn't do a fresh playthrough until Sunday morning. Some friction points that would have been obvious on Friday were still there at submission.
  • Keep a list of cuts. We cut things verbally during the jam. A written list would have made it easier to revisit them if time opened up.

Final Thoughts

Ludum Dare is one of the best things I do every year. The constraints force decisions. The deadline makes shipping real. And shipping something — anything — teaches you more about game design than any amount of planning.

Camp Ketosis isn't perfect. But it's a complete experience you can sit down and play, and we made it in a weekend. That's still kind of incredible to me every time.
